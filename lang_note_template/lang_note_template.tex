\vspace{4pt}

\begin{contentbox}
In the realm of English literature, effective note-taking is a crucial skill that aids in comprehension, analysis, and retention of complex literary works. A well-structured note-taking system allows students to dissect novels, poems, and plays into manageable segments, facilitating a deeper understanding of themes, characters, and literary devices. Strategies such as summarizing key plot points, identifying recurring motifs, and tracing character development are fundamental. Furthermore, noting contextual information---historical background, authorial intent, and critical reception---enriches the interpretative process. Incorporating visual aids, such as mind maps or timelines, can also enhance recall and provide a holistic view of the literary piece. Ultimately, the goal of effective note-taking is to transform passive reading into an active, engaging process, empowering students to construct their own interpretations and insights.

% ═══ Section: Research and Analyze Sources ════════════
\begin{sectionheader}
\emoji{🔎} \sectionfont{Research and Analyze Sources}
\end{sectionheader}

\vspace{4pt}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=2pt, topsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Primary sources:} These are original materials that provide firsthand accounts or direct evidence concerning a topic. In English literature, this includes the novels, poems, plays, and essays written by authors being studied. These are the foundation of literary analysis.
  \item \textbf{Secondary sources:} These sources offer analysis, interpretation, or evaluation of primary sources. They often provide a broader perspective by synthesizing information. Examples include literary criticism, scholarly articles, book reviews, and analytical essays.
  \item \textbf{Tertiary sources:} These sources compile and summarize information from primary and secondary sources. They are often used for gaining an overview or for reference. Examples include encyclopedias, textbooks, and literary guides.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{4pt}

\begin{tipbox}
\emoji{💡} \textbf{Research Tip:} When doing research, look for primary and secondary sources from different places. Read books, websites, and articles. Ask: Is this information true? Does it make sense? Use what you learn to answer your questions.
\end{tipbox}
